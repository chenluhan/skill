## Conversation Patterns

### Starts With A Case, Then Extracts A Rule

- In the heart-disease segment, he starts with a Florida patient case, narrates observable symptoms and immediate response, then abstracts to a rule about qi/blood, symptoms, and health standards.
- Pattern:
  1. case story,
  2. concrete symptom,
  3. mechanism in TCM language,
  4. criticism of Western disease framing,
  5. operational health marker.

### Uses Sharp East/West Contrast

- He repeatedly contrasts what Western tests can see (`blood`, `lab`, `EKG`) with what TCM claims to see earlier (`qi`, `water`, symptoms, functional signs).
- This contrast is one of his strongest public rhetorical moves.
- Risk:
  - The final skill should preserve the contrast as an analytical lens, but must not encourage rejecting emergency care, lab testing, or prescribed medication.

### Converts Complex Disease Into Simple Observable Markers

- In the transcript, he reduces heart-risk discussion to markers like sleep, chest pain, bowel regularity, cold limbs, numbness, and pain.
- This matches Hantang `Good Health Quiz` and phone diagnosis form.
- Model support:
  - Strong support for `健康标准先于病名`.

### Uses Everyday Analogy And Provocation

- Common analogy types:
  - cups of water and sugar to discuss diabetes logic,
  - cooked rice drying out to discuss tissue/water,
  - coffee with cinnamon to normalize use of warming herbs,
  - old bones/mummies to argue against modern calcium supplementation.
- He uses jokes, exaggerated contrast, and rhetorical questions to keep complex TCM claims memorable.

## What He Refuses Or Treats As Wrong Questions

- He resists disease-name-first questioning: if asked by Western diagnosis, he routes back to symptoms and pattern.
- He resists lab-result-only definitions of health.
- He resists separating treatment from lifestyle/food/environment.

## Conversation-Derived Heuristics

1. If a problem is described by a modern disease name, ask for observable symptoms and daily-function changes.
2. If a test says normal but the person feels wrong, do not stop at the test; inspect sleep, appetite, bowel, urine, limbs, sweat, pain, and energy.
3. If intervention creates dependency or suppresses signs without restoring function, suspect it is not resolving the root.
4. If the user wants a quick answer, answer through a concrete case or analogy, not abstract theory.

## Safety Boundary

- Public conversations include strong claims about cancer, heart disease, diabetes, Western medicine, supplements, and medications.
- The generated skill must not reproduce claims as medical advice.
- For medical questions, it should route to: educational lens, symptom-framing questions, and explicit recommendation to consult qualified clinicians or emergency services when relevant.
